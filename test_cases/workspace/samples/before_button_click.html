Abstract:
This paper reports the world's first field trial of a 100-km reach symmetric-rate ten gigabit Ethernet passive optical network (10G-EPON) system that uses optical-amplifier-based PON repeaters. Semiconductor optical amplifiers (SOAs) are utilized to handle the 10G-EPON wavelength allocation defined in IEEE802.3av; a fast automatic level control technique is applied to cascaded SOAs for upstream amplification to achieve high gain and wide input dynamic range. An electronic dispersion compensation technique is also applied for downstream detection. These techniques support a 79.5-dB loss budget and 100-km reach transmission with 40 km span lengths. In a field trial, we successfully demonstrate bidirectional real-time uncompressed high-definition television signal transmission over installed standard single-mode fiber links.

SECTION I.

Introduction

The explosive growth of the Internet has triggered the introduction of broadband access and continuous increases in content bandwidth and Internet use frequency are still accelerating the bandwidth demands placed on access networks. To meet this demand, fiber-to-the-home (FTTH) is being deployed in some countries instead of conventional access networks via twisted-pair copper cables. Passive optical networks (PONs) based on a point-to-multipoint topology are often utilized to provide customers with broadband services cost effectively because a feeder optical fiber cable and a photonic aggregation equipment at a central office (CO) are shared by several tens of customers. Recently, IEEE standardized gigabit Ethernet PONs (G-EPONs) and ITU-T standardized gigabit-capable PONs (G-PONs) have provided users with Gb/s-class bandwidth services through the use of these shared access system [1], [2]. Although these PONs are reliable and the number of users has been steadily growing, there is a great demand for improving the acceptable link budget of access systems to offer PON-based services to more customers more effectively. To meet this demand, some research groups have been studying the application of optical amplifiers given their advantages of transparency to frame format and signal bit rate, and simple management. They have developed novel optical amplifiers consisting of semiconductor optical amplifiers (SOAs) [3], [4], Raman amplifiers (RA) [5], or optical fiber amplifiers (OFAs) [6], [7] for PON applications, and intensively investigated the system performance of optically amplified PONs [8][9][10][11][12].

In the last few years, ten gigabit Ethernet passive optical network (10G-EPON) (both symmetric and asymmetric line rates) and XG-PON (only asymmetric line rate) have been standardized in IEEE and ITU-T, respectively, toward the realization of post GE-PON and G-PON applications and several developments based on these specifications have been also reported [13][14][15]. Of particular note, the authors of [13] reported a field trial that uses asymmetric-rate XG-PON equipment to deliver 10 Gb/s downstream and 2.5 Gb/s upstream. Other papers have introduced 10 Gb/s-class long-reach/high-splitting ratio PONs that use optical amplifiers [16] [18] [19] [20] [21]; however, very few papers have reported the system performance possible with 10 Gb/s-class PON equipment [22].

In this paper, we demonstrate the first field trial of 100-km reach transmission using symmetric-rate 10G-EPON equipment and PON repeaters based on SOAs, to deliver upstream (1270 nm) and downstream (1577 nm) signals. A fast automatic level controlled (ALC) circuit is implemented between cascaded SOAs to achieve high gain and wide input dynamic range for upstream signal amplification and an electronic dispersion compensation (EDC) technique is applied for downstream signal detection.

SECTION II.

Long-Reach PON System Using SOA-Based PON Repeater With ALC Function

A. System Configuration

Fig. 1 shows the configuration of a long-reach PON system that uses optical-amplifier-based PON repeaters. An optical line terminal (OLT) at the CO is connected to each optical network unit (ONU) at the customer's home through an optical splitter. Several PON repeaters are also installed between the OLT and the optical splitter. Here, access span and trunk span refer to the span between the ONUs and Repeater #1 and that between Repeater #1 and the OLT, respectively. The access span corresponds to an optical distribution network (ODN) of the normal PON systems and the distance of the PON ODN is extended by the trunk span. Optical amplifiers with high gain can greatly improve the link budget of each span, and extend the reach from the OLT to the farthest ONU or reduce the number of PON repeaters required. However, upstream amplification is more difficult than downstream amplification due to the bursty nature of the signals being amplified; excessively strong signal inputs from the nearest ONU severely degrade signal quality due to the gain response delays of the optical amplifiers. To successfully relay burst signals transmitted in the PON ODN across the extended trunk span, we apply an ALC technique to the cascaded optical amplifiers for upstream amplification implemented in Repeater #1. As optical amplifiers, we use SOAs to handle the wavelength assignment of 10G-EPON systems. Details of Repeater #1 are described in the next section.

B. SOA-Based PON Repeater With an ALC Function

Fig. 2 shows the configuration of the SOA-based PON repeater that we developed as Repeater #1 in Fig. 1 [23]. It consists of fast ALC-SOAs for burst-mode upstream amplification, an ALC-SOA for continuous-mode downstream amplification, and two wavelength division multiplexing (WDM) filters to multi/demultiplex upstream and downstream signals. The fast ALC-SOAs consist of two SOAs, a course-WDM (CWDM) filter, and a fast ALC circuit that supports burst-mode operation. The first SOA boosts the input power levels to meet the input power ranges of the fast ALC circuit. The fast ALC circuit [18], where a high-speed variable optical attenuator (VOA) is driven with both feed-forward (FF) and feed-back (FB) control circuits, is placed after the CWDM filter to keep the input power levels to the second booster SOA constant regardless of the input power levels to the first SOA. To improve system robustness in the face of long consecutive runs of identical digits (CIDs), we modified the algorithm installed in the FF control circuit in the ALC; the input power level to the ALC circuit is judged from average power level only in the preamble of each burst signal sequence. The response time of this ALC circuit is around 50 ns. This arrangement prevents the second SOA from triggering the pattern effect and contributes to the realization of high gain and wide input dynamic range. Fig. 3 shows the measured signal traces for the fast ALC-SOAs input and output. We set the input powers from two ONUs to 10.0 and 28.0 dBm, respectively. The burst signal pattern is the same as that described in the next section. As shown in the figure, the fast ALC circuit suppresses the power difference of 18 dB to less than 1 dB; the output powers are around 4 dBm. On the other hand, the ALC-SOA consists of a SOA and a FF control circuit. Unlike upstream amplification, we use a single SOA for downstream amplification since the trunk span losses are much lower in downstream transmission than those of the access span in upstream transmission due to the wavelength dependence of optical fibers and nonexistence of optical splitters with a large number of splitting ratio before amplification. The ALC function is achieved by controlling SOA driving current and is used to hold the output powers from the SOAs to those from the OLT and keep input powers to the ONUs within the input power range of their receivers. Note that this function does not need to support burst-mode operation. Fig. 4 shows the relationship between input and output powers of the ALC-SOA, where the target output power levels are set to 6, 4, and 2 dBm. As shown in the figure, the output power levels of around 6, 4, and 2 dBm are achieved against input powers to ALC-SOA over 10.5, 12.5, and 14.5 dBm, respectively.

This PON repeater has the ability to support 60-km reach 10G-EPON systems; however, it is obvious that it does not support 100 km transmission at the wavelength of 1270 nm. To realize the 100-km reach field trial, we prepared a second repeater (Repeater #2).

SECTION III.

Loss BDGET Evaluation in a 100-km Reach 10G-EPON System

A. Upstream Transmission

Fig. 5 shows the experimental setup used to measure allowable loss budgets of the upstream link in a 100-km reach PON system consisting of an 80 km trunk span with 40 km span length and a 20 km access span; it replicates the field trial configuration. It consists of two SOA-based repeaters and a 10G-EPON system. Repeater #1 configuration is the same as that shown in Fig. 2. On the other hand, Repeater #2 consists of a single SOA for upstream (no level control) and the ALC-SOA for downstream. Our 10G-EPON system accommodates multiple ONUs with fiber spans ranging from 0 to 100 km. To support 100 km, the dynamic bandwidth allocation (DBA) cycle is set to 1500 s. The upstream and downstream signals have the bit rate of 10.3125 Gb/s. Here, each sequence contains a preamble of 512 ns and 20% of full line rate is loaded with 64 byte frames by using a traffic analyzer. Here, we use two ONUs to emulate strong (ONU #1) and weak (ONU #2) burst signals. The output powers and the extinction ratios (ERs) of ONU #1 and ONU #2 are 5.3 dBm/6.6 dB and 4.7 dBm/7.2 dB, respectively. The center wavelengths are 1271.9 and 1271.8 nm, respectively. The receiver sensitivity at the bit error rate (BER)=1012 of the OLT is 28.0 dBm. Here, forward error correction (FEC), RS (255, 223), is applied to meet the 10G-EPON specifications defined in IEEE802.3av. The downstream signals are dropped by three WDM filters and an optical splitter to evaluate only upstream transmission. Losses of the access span are adjusted by VOA #1 and VOA #2 and span losses of trunk span are adjusted by VOA #3 and VOA #4. We measure frame error rates (FERs) while changing the loss values of both access and trunk spans and the obtained data are converted to BERs by simple calculations: the FERs are divided by the extended Ethernet frame size of 68 byte including a 4 byte virtual local area network (VLAN) tag.

Fig. 5.

Experimental setup to measure allowable loss budget of upstream transmission in a 100-km reach 10G-EPON system.

Fig. 6 shows BER versus input power to the fast ALC-SOAs; span losses of the trunk span range from 17 to 24 dB. Here, the span loss of 17 dB is obtained by setting attenuation range of VOA #3 and VOA #4 to zero, which is almost identical to the losses of 40 km standard single mode fibers (SMFs). The input powers to the fast ALC-SOAs from ONU #1 were fixed to 10.0 dBm and only those from ONU #2 were changed. As shown in the figure, the minimum input power to the fast ALC-SOAs was 28.5 dBm and the wide dynamic range of 18.5 dB was achieved. Taking ONU output power of 4.7 dBm into consideration, the loss budget of the upstream access span is estimated to be 33.2 dB.

B. Downstream Transmission

Fig. 7 shows the experimental setup used to measure the allowable loss downstream budgets in a 100-km reach PON system. In each PON repeater, only the ALC-SOA for downstream amplification is used. The target output power level is set to 4 dBm, which meets the OLT output power range, 2--5 dBm, defined in IEEE802.3av. The output power, the ER, and the central wavelength of the OLT are 2.5 dBm, 9.0 dB, and 1577.3 nm, respectively. The ONU receiver sensitivity is 31.4 dBm. The upstream signals are dropped by two WDM filters to evaluate only downstream transmission. Span losses of two trunk span are adjusted by VOA #5 and VOA #6 and losses of access span are adjusted by VOA #7.

Fig. 7.

Experimental setup to measure allowable loss budget of downstream transmission in a 100-km reach 10G-EPON system.

In long-reach PON systems, the downstream signals of a 10G-EPON are severely degraded due to the chromatic dispersion of the SMFs. Therefore, the ONU must employ a chromatic dispersion compensation technique [24] if the receiver sensitivity of downstream transmission is to meet the specifications defined in IEEE802.3av in long-reach PON systems. To meet this demand, we developed an external EDC box and place it in front of ONU #2 since no dispersion compensation devices are implemented in our ONUs. Fig. 8 shows the configuration of the EDC box. As shown in the figure, while the upstream signals are directly launched into the access span through two WDM filters, the downstream signals are sent to each ONU using electrical to optical (E/O) conversion after detection at the EDC box by an avalanche photodiode with a trans-impedance amplifier (APD-TIA), and an EDC with 16-tap feed forward equalization (FFE) and 8-state maximum likelihood sequence detection (MLSD), and clock data recovery (CDR). Fig. 9 shows the measured receiver sensitivities of the EDC box. As shown in the figure, while the sensitivities of the ONU receiver are significantly degraded as optical fiber length increases, those of the EDC box remain constant and satisfy the IEEE802.3av receiver sensitivity of 28.5 dBm even with 100 km.

The figure also shows the loss budgets given as the power differences between receiver sensitivities and output powers. As shown in the figure, the output powers from the ALC-SOA in Repeater #1 exceed 2 dBm, which is the minimum setting of the OLT output power, against span loss values from 17 to 24 dB. Actually, the output power at the span loss value of 24 dB is 2.7 dBm, which is 1.3 dB lower than the target output power level of 4 dBm. This is because the corresponding span loss at 1577 nm is about 16.5 dB and the input power to each ALC-SOA (about 14 dBm) falls below the input power level (12.5 dBm) at which the ALC function works properly (see Fig. 4). On the other hand, despite the amplified spontaneous emission (ASE) noise accumulation in the downstream SOAs, the receiver sensitivities exhibit almost constant values, under 28 dBm, against the same span loss range. This is because the input powers to the ALC-SOA for downstream amplification are much larger than those to the fast ALC-SOAs for upstream amplification. As a result, the figure also shows that the loss budget of the downstream access span is 31.5 dB, which is 1.7 dB lower than that of upstream access span; thus, it dominates the loss budget in bidirectional transmission. Summing this value and the span loss of 24 dB (two spans) yields the total system loss budget of 79.5 dB.

C. Bidirectional Transmission

To confirm the feasibility of the 100-km reach field trial, we also conducted a bidirectional transmission experiment using the experimental setup shown in Fig. 11. Input powers to the fast ALC-SOAs in Repeater #1 from ONU #1 and ONN #2 were set to 10.0 and 27.0 dBm, respectively, and the span losses of the both trunk spans were set to 17 and 24 dB. With these conditions, we successfully demonstrated error-free transmission in both directions. Fig. 12 summarizes the level diagram between OLT and ONU #2 at the span loss of 24 dB.

SECTION IV.

Field Trial

We conducted a 100-km reach field trial in Hokkaido Prefecture. The field

location is shown in Fig. 13. Hokkaido is Japan's second largest island and comprises 20% of Japan's total island area. Since its population density is lower than those of the other 46 prefectures, providing FTTH services remains a major issue in most areas. Fig. 14 shows the field trial configuration; it passes through Sapporo (A), Eniwa (B), Chitose (C), and Toyohira (D) buildings. The 100 km distance consists of a total of 90 km of installed SMFs and a 10 km SMF bobbin in Sapporo building. A double-star topology with an 8-way split-way optical splitter is formed by an optical fiber loop originating in Sapporo. An OLT, two ONUs, a PON Repeater #1 operated in burst mode, two EDC boxes, and high-definition television (HDTV) equipment are centralized in Sapporo, while PON Repeater #2 is located in Chitose. Real-time images taken by an HDTV camera are sent to an HDTV encoder/decoder, converted to Internet Protocol (IP) signals without compression, and output through two GbE ports. A layer-2 (L2) switch converts two 1GbE ports to one 10 GbE port since our OLT and ONU have 10 GbE interfaces. The encoded signals, whose bandwidth is about 1.7 Gb/s, are bidirectionally transmitted between the OLT and the ONU and are displayed on HDTV monitor screens after being decoded by the HDTV encoder/decoder. The input power to Repeater #1 from one of ONUs for weak signals is adjusted to 27.0 dBm by a VOA. Although an 8-way optical splitter was used, this attenuation setting supports 32-way splitters. A frame loss test confirmed error-free transmission with this configuration. When we think about video system performance, transmission latency is also a critical factor in achieving adequate service quality. Fig. 15 shows measured latencies versus frame length for downstream and upstream transmission. As shown in the figure, acceptable latencies, about 500 s for downstream and about 2800 s for upstream, are achieved regardless of frame length. The picture in Fig. 16 shows a scene of the demonstration in Sapporo. As shown, we successfully demonstrated bidirectional real-time uncompressed HDTV signal transmission during office hours (8 h).

SECTION V.

Conclusion

We have presented a 79.5-dB loss budget, 100-km reach symmetric-rate 10G-EPON system using ALC burst-mode SOAs with the wide input dynamic range of 18.5 dB for upstream transmission and EDC circuits for downstream transmission. The success of a field trial held in Hokkaido using the same system promises future PON systems that can accommodate more users (long-reach, high-splitting ratios, etc.) in a cost-effective manner.
